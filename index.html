The Impressionist Revolution: Monet to Matisse

from the Dallas Museum of Art

The art style of impressionism was birthed in 1874 and impacted twentieth-century art since. The artist who rebelled against traditional academic painting created a new visual language that emphasized light, color, and the fleeting effects of nature. These artists, known as impressionists, redefined the cutting edge of artistic expression and comtemporary art. Join us as the Frist Museum explores this revolutionary movement with pieces from core impressionists Edgar Degas, Claude Monet, and Berthe Morisot. Then the avant-garde artists they inspired, Pual Gauguin, Vincent van Gogh, Piet mondrian, and Henri Matisse. See nearly 50 paintings and sculptures that revolutionized the art world.

Impressionism's rebellious origins

In the mid-1800's, the Paris art scene was all about making artwork that favored historical, mythological, and religious subjects in precise detail. This was very constraining on young artists. They wanted to break free from these rules and paint what they saw in everyday life, using loose brushstrokes and vibrant colors to capture the momentary effects of light and atmosphere. This was life that everyone could relate to, not the lofty and idealized subjects of the academies.

The key characteristics of Impressionism include visible brushstrokes, everyday life scenes, an emphasis on light, bold and vibrant colors, often painted side by side without blending. With the invention of portable paint tubes, artists could now paint outdoors (en plein air), capturing the changing effects of light and atmosphere in real-time. This allowed them to experiment with new techniques and perspectives, leading to a fresh and dynamic approach to art.

Their work was met with rejection early on. Claude Monet's painting "Impression, Sunrise" was ridiculed by critics saying it looked like an "impression" rather than a finished piece. However, the term was embraced by the artists themselves and "Impressionism" was born.

Impact on modern art

Their work started a precedent to challenge academic traditions, broadening what art can depict, to eventually the fundamental level. This was the beginning of artist showing their personal vision and perception of life and the world.

Impressionism paved the way for various modern art movements, including Post-Impressionism, Fauvism, and Cubism. Artists like Vincent van Gogh, Paul Gauguin, Henri Matisse, and Pablo Picasso drew inspiration from the Impressionists' innovative techniques and approaches to color and composition.

Today, Impressionism remains one of the most beloved and influential art movements in history. Its emphasis on capturing the fleeting moments of everyday life continues to resonate with audiences worldwide, reminding us of the beauty and transience of our own experiences.

Featured Artists

Edgar Degas

Edgar Degas is known for his paintings of ballet dancers and urban scenes.

Claude Monet

Claude Monet is famous for his series of paintings depicting the same scene at different times of day.

Berthe Morisot

Berthe Morisot was a key figure in the Impressionist movement, known for her intimate domestic scenes.

Camille Pissarro

Camille Pissarro was a French Impressionist artist who played a crucial role in the development of the movement.

Paul Gauguin

Paul Gauguin was a French Post-Impressionist artist known for his bold colors and symbolic imagery.

Henri Matisse

Henri Matisse was a French artist known for his use of color and his fluid, expressive brushwork.

Piet Mondrian

Piet Mondrian was a Dutch painter known for his abstract compositions and geometric forms.

Vincent van Gogh

Vincent van Gogh was a Dutch Post-Impressionist painter known for his expressive use of color and brushstroke.
